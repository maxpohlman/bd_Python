Chengjin Chu, John Norman, Robert Flynn, Nicole Kaplan, William K. Lauenroth, Peter B. Adler. 2013. Cover, density, and demographics of shortgrass steppe plants mapped 1997–2010 in permanent grazed and ungrazed quadrats. Ecology 94:1435. http://dx.doi.org/10.1890/13-0121.1

Introduction

"Chart quadrats" are permanent 1-m² quadrats in which all individual plants are identified and mapped using a pantograph tracing tool that creates a scaled-down representation of an individual plant's shape and location on paper (Hill 1920). The species of each individual is determined and then labeled on the paper. Under Clements' (1907) influence, many range experiment stations across the western U.S. began mapping quadrats between the 1910s and the 1930s and continued annual censuses for decades (White 1985). We recently have published historical data sets from southern mixed-prairie in western Kansas (Adler et al. 2007), sagebrush-steppe in eastern Idaho (Zachmann et al. 2010), northern mixed-prairie in eastern Montana (Anderson et al. 2011), and Sonoran desert in Arizona (Anderson et al. 2012). The data set described here, from shortgrass steppe in Colorado, contains 24 quadrats mapped annually from 1997 to 2010. These new data will support more robust cross-site comparisons, especially because many species common in the shortgrass steppe also occur in the Montana (northern mixed prairie) and Kansas (southern mixed prairie) data sets.

Chart quadrat data are unique in several ways. First, the fine spatial resolution of the maps makes it possible to track the fates of individual plants, providing detailed demographic information that is rare for herbaceous plants (Lauenroth and Adler 2008). Such demographic information is often essential for understanding community and ecosystem patterns and will be important for predicting how plant populations and communities respond to climate change (Adler et al. 2012). Second, the maps enable analysis of spatial patterns and interactions among plants in local neighborhoods (e.g., Purves and Law 2002). Third, the long-term nature of the data can reveal temporal variability in demographic performance and spatial interactions. And finally, these data are available for almost all species in the community.

Contemporary chart quadrat analyses use Geographic Information Systems and modern statistical techniques to take advantage of a unique combination of long-term, individual-level data for the whole community. We have automated analyses of the survival, life expectancies, and life spans of perennial grassland plants (Lauenroth and Adler 2008). Such demographic data can then be used to address additional research questions about the factors driving plant population dynamics (Adler and HilleRisLambers 2008, Dalgleish et al. 2011) and mechanisms of species coexistence (Adler et al. 2006, 2009, 2010).

Unlike most chart quadrat data sets, which were initiated early in the 20th century, the shortgrass steppe study was initiated by contemporary researchers in 1997 and quadrats were censused for 14 years, with the last census in 2010. Because it was initiated in the era of modern experimental design, the shortgrass data set will enable robust tests of the effect of livestock grazing on population and community dynamics. Although some of the historical data sets included quadrats in both grazed and ungrazed areas, replication was often poor or unbalanced and questions remain about whether the quadrat locations represent random sampling. The shortgrass steppe data set suffers none of these issues and goes beyond a simple grazed vs. ungrazed contrast to include long-term ungrazed quadrats recently opened to grazing, and long-term grazed quadrats where grazing was recently excluded. Thus, the shortgrass steppe data set provides unparalleled opportunities to study grazing effects on plant demographic rates, interactions between annual fluctuations in weather and grazing, and the influence of grazing on plant-plant interactions.

The data set contains the following data and data formats: (1) the digitized maps in shapefile format; (2) a tabular representation of centroid or point location (x, y coordinates), and basal cover (m²) for plants mapped as polygons; (3) quadrat information including location (longitude and latitude), elevation, and livestock grazing treatment and history; (4) detailed information on stocking rates during the period of quadrat sampling; (5) daily temperature and precipitation; (6) a species list including the total records for each species, plant growth forms and longevity; and (7) an inventory of the years each quadrat was sampled.

Principal Investigator: William Lauenroth

Abstract: This data set was created to map and analyze demographic rates of many common plant species in the shortgrass steppe of North America under grazed and ungrazed conditions. Twenty-four permanent 1-m² quadrats were established as part of the Shortgrass Steppe Long Term Ecological Research project in six grazed and ungrazed study sites on the Central Plains Experimental Range in Nunn, Colorado, USA. We used a pantograph to record the location and shape of individual plants annually from 1997 to 2010. These data make it possible to determine the fate of individual plants using a Geographic Information System and spatial analysis techniques. The data provide unique opportunities to test the interactive effects of grazing and climate variables on demographic rates, plant–plant interactions, and population and community dynamics. We provide the following data and data formats: (1) the digitized maps in shapefile format; (2) a tabular representation of centroid or point location (x, y coordinates), and basal cover (m²) for plants mapped as polygons; (3) quadrat information including location (longitude and latitude), elevation, and livestock grazing treatment and history; (4) detailed information on stocking rates during the period of quadrat sampling; (5) daily temperature and precipitation values; (6) a species list including the total records for each species, plant growth forms, and longevity; and (7) an inventory of the years each quadrat was sampled.

Class II. Research origin descriptors

A. Overall project description

The SGS-LTER site is charged to addresses themes and questions relevant to water-limited, low-producitivty systems, and grazing. Exploring the potential effects of grazing on individuals, populations and communities is the main motivation for collecting this long-term data set. We followed the growth patterns of individual plants under different grazing regimes. These data, and accompanying weather and grazing information, may be applied to population studies to ask how survival, growth, and mortality depend on grazing, climate variability, and plant–plant interactions. Community studies can aggregate the individual level data to explore how these drivers influence species composition and diversity through time.

B. Specific subproject description

1. Site description:

The SGS-LTER site encompasses a large portion of the Colorado Piedmont Section of the western Great Plains. The site is located 61 km east of Fort Collins and 40 km northeast of Greeley in Weld County, northeastern Colorado, and extends 90 km east to include the entire the Pawnee National Grasslands (PNG). The CPER is a contiguous 6280 ha block of land. The PNG is 78,100 ha of public land that lies within an approximate 50 by 100 km checkerboard of public, private and State of Colorado lands.

The main natural plant communities are shortgrass steppe, floodplain shrubland, and salt meadow. The ecosystem is dominated by short grasses (64%), succulents (21%) and dwarf shrubs (8%). Long-lived C4 grasses such as blue grama dominate under the characteristically dry conditions of the shortgrass steppe by efficiently accessing available water.

The shortgrass steppe is unique among North American grasslands for its long history of grazing by large herbivores and periodic drought. Over time, intense selection by grazing and drought has created an ecosystem that is well adapted to both, with low-stature vegetation and biological activity and organic matter concentrated below ground. Currently, grazing by domestic livestock is the primary land use of native grassland, which occupies about 60% of the land area of the shortgrass steppe.

You can find more information about the study site here: http://sgslter.colostate.edu/.

2. Experimental and sampling design

a. Design characteristics: In 1997, 6 pastures with historical exclosures and grazing treatments were selected (pastures numbered as 19, 11, 24, 7, 5a, 5b). Each pasture included an old exclosure that was ungrazed in the past and continues to be ungrazed at present (ungrazed/ungrazed or unun treatment) and areas grazed by livestock in the past and present (grazed/grazed or gzgz treatment). In 1991, two new grazing treatments were added. A new fence was installed to encompass part of the grazed/grazed (gzgz) area, creating a grazed/ungrazed (gzun) treatment, and a section of the ungrazed/ungrazed (unun) exclosure was opened to grazing, creating an ungrazed/grazed (ungz) treatment. In each pasture, we maintained one permanent quadrat in each treatment area (for example, gzgz_5a, unun_5a, gzun_5a, and ungz_5a). This effort resulted in digitized maps for all 24 permanent quadrats. Detailed information on stocking rates during the sampling period is described in the file quad_stocking_rate.csv.

b.Permanentplots:1 m² quadrats.

c. Data collection: Quadrats were mapped annually from 1997 to 2010, but some records were lost for several quadrats in 2000 (see the file quad_inventory.csv). Quadrats were typically mapped in June every year and always in the same order (19, 11, 24, 7, 5a, and 5b).

3. Research Methods

a. Field / laboratory: The data were collected in the field using pantographs (Hill 1920), a mechanical device used to make scale drawings. The original paper maps were first scanned and then stored as JPEG image files with a naming convention to contain pasture and grazing treatment. These images were then converted into shapefiles by heads-up digitization in ArcGIS version 9.3. Layers of points were incorporated into the GIS using the editor toolbar and sketchtool in ArcMap and the species information was added to the attribute table. Layers of lines were sketched and created and then converted to polygons with the ArcToolbox. Species information was added to the attribute table associated with the polygon layer.

b. Instrumentation: Pantographs, scanners, and computers running ArcGIS and R.

c. Taxonomy and systematics: Originally assigned plant names were corrected for synonyms based on the USDA Plants Database (http://plants.usda.gob/). We recorded these name changes in the species_name_changes.csv file.

 

Class III. Data set status and accessibility

A. Status

1. Data verification: After the initial digitizing phase, all maps were checked for completeness and accuracy.

a. Layers were double-checked for missing points and polygons.

b. Shapefiles were rotated to have a consistent orientation;

c. Species names for unlabeled and obviously mislabeled polygons and points were assigned based on the species names of the same features in previous and later years;

d. Shapefiles were processed using R and Python scripts to clip the polygon and point features at the map borders and remove any small polygon “slivers” generated accidentally while digitizing;

e. Other miscellaneous corrections based on visual inspection of the shapefiles;

f. All species were then classified as either density- or cover-type features. Density-type species, typically forbs, were consistently mapped as points. Cover-type species, typically perennial grasses, were consistently mapped as polygons. Records of cover-type species mapped as points were converted to arbitrary small square polygons of 0.25 cm2. Density-type species mapped as polygons were converted to point features with location at the centroid of the polygon. Shrubs usually mapped by both points and polygons were kept unchanged;

g. Plants names were corrected for synonyms based on the USDA PLANTS Dataset (http://plants.usda.gov/); and

h. x, y coordinates of each polygon centroid were added to shapefile attribute tables.

Class IV. Data structural descriptors

A. Data Set File

1. Identity: shapefiles.zip

2. Size: 28,371 Kb (compressed)

3. Format and storage mode: Shapefiles compressed and submitted together in a zipped directory.

4. Header information: The fields within the attributes tables for each shapefile are described in the tabular data, see “Records of all individual plants mapped as points” and “Records of all individual plants mapped as polygons,” for the density and cover shapefiles, respectively.

B. Variable information

This is a zipped directory, containing every individual shapefile for each year that each quadrat was mapped. File names reflect the quadrat, year (YYYY), and geometry (pnt or poly) of each shapefile. “poly” refers to “cover” (features mapped as polygons) while “pnt” refers to “density” (features mapped as points). For example, “pnt_gzgz_5a_1997.shp” is the point shapefile for year 1997 in quadrat gzgz_5a. Each feature in these shapefiles has attributes that describe the individual, such as species name and location within the quadrat. Note that we removed shapefiles that contained no features from the dataset (e.g. if no forbs occurred in a quadrat in a given year, we removed the empty density shapefile).

Class V. Supplemental descriptors

A. Data acquisition

1. Data entry verification procedures: See II. and III. above.

B. Quality assurance/quality control procedures: The procedures above (II. and III.) ensured accurate transfer of information from the original maps. Nevertheless, future users must become familiar enough with the raw data provided here to determine whether or not it is appropriate for their particular research question. One data quality issue warrants special attention: we noticed mapping inconsistencies involving species Bouteloua gracilis and Buchloe dactyloides inquadrats gzgz_24, gzun_24, ungz_24, and gzun_24, which is understandable since these two grasses can be difficult to distinguish in the field. We were unable to resolve the true species identity of these polygons. We have more confidence in the data from the other quadrats.
